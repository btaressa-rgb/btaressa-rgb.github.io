Hypertext History Timeline

Home > History > Timeline

A chronological overview of key events in the history of hypertext and the World Wide Web, from Vannevar Bush's visionary 1945 article to the public launch of the Web.

  1. 1945
    Vannevar Bush publishes "As We May Think" in The Atlantic Monthly, proposing the MEMEX — a mechanical device to augment human memory through automated cross-referencing of documents.
  2. 1963
    Ted Nelson coins the term "hypertext" to describe non-sequential writing — text that branches and allows the reader to choose their own path.
  3. 1965
    Ted Nelson presents his hypertext concept at the ACM conference. He begins work on Project Xanadu, envisioning a universal hypertext repository.
  4. 1968
    Douglas Engelbart demonstrates the oN-Line System (NLS) at the "Mother of All Demos," showcasing hypertext linking, collaborative editing, and the computer mouse.
  5. 1980
    Tim Berners-Lee writes ENQUIRE — a personal database program at CERN that uses hypertext-style links between nodes, a precursor to the World Wide Web.
  6. 1987
    Apple releases HyperCard, bringing hypertext to millions of Macintosh users and popularizing the concept of linked cards and stacks.
  7. 1989
    Tim Berners-Lee submits his proposal "Information Management: A Proposal" to CERN management, outlining a distributed hypertext system for the internet. His supervisor Mike Sendall writes "Vague but exciting" on the cover.
  8. 1990
    Tim Berners-Lee and Robert Cailliau formalize the proposal for the World Wide Web. Berners-Lee writes the first web browser (WorldWideWeb) and the first web server (httpd) on a NeXT computer at CERN.
  9. 1991
    The World Wide Web is made available on the internet outside CERN. The first website goes live at info.cern.ch. The line-mode browser is released for non-NeXT platforms.
  10. 1993
    NCSA Mosaic is released, the first graphical web browser, dramatically increasing public access to the World Wide Web. CERN announces that the Web will be free and open to all.
