A Short History of the Web

Home > History > WWW Project History

The World Wide Web was invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 while working at CERN, the European Particle Physics Laboratory in Geneva, Switzerland. What began as a proposal for managing information grew into a global information system that transformed human communication.

Origins at CERN

In March 1989, Tim Berners-Lee submitted a proposal titled "Information Management: A Proposal" to CERN management. The proposal described a distributed information system using hypertext to link documents across the network. His supervisor, Mike Sendall, wrote "Vague but exciting" on the cover page — and approved further development.

The problem Berners-Lee was trying to solve was practical: CERN employed thousands of researchers who needed to share information across different computers, operating systems, and networks. Existing systems required users to learn different programs and procedures on different computers. A universal system was needed.

The First Proposal (1989)

The original proposal outlined a system with the following key characteristics:

  • A distributed hypertext system accessible over the internet
  • Documents linked by hypertext references
  • A universal addressing scheme for all documents
  • A simple protocol for transferring documents between computers
  • A markup language for creating structured documents

These ideas became the foundations of HTTP, URLs, and HTML respectively.

Development (1990–1991)

In October 1990, the project was formally approved. By December 1990, Berners-Lee had written the first web browser (called "WorldWideWeb") and the first web server (httpd) on a NeXT workstation. The first website was hosted at info.cern.ch.

In 1991, the line-mode browser was released, making the Web accessible on any computer with a terminal. See the timeline and timetable for a detailed chronology.

Public Release and Growth

In August 1991, Berners-Lee posted an announcement about the World Wide Web on the alt.hypertext newsgroup, making it available to the public internet. By November 1992, there were 26 web servers worldwide. By October 1993, there were over 500.

In April 1993, CERN made a landmark decision: the World Wide Web technology would be placed in the public domain, free for anyone to use. This decision was crucial to the Web's explosive growth.

Legacy

The World Wide Web has fundamentally transformed how humanity accesses, shares, and creates information. From its origins as a document management system at a physics laboratory, it has grown into the primary medium for global communication, commerce, education, and culture.

Tim Berners-Lee went on to found the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) in 1994 to develop open standards for the Web, ensuring it remains an open and universal platform.
